Task 1.a¶

Compute the Fourier transform for the function: $f(x) = x e^{-\alpha |x|}, \alpha > 0$

$$F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f(x) e^{-j \omega x} d x=\int_{-\infty}^{0} x e^{\alpha x} e^{-j \omega x} d x+\int_{0}^{+\infty} x e^{-\alpha x} e^{-j \omega x} d x = I_{1} + I_{2}$$

$$ I_{1}=\int_{-\infty}^{0} x e^{(\alpha-j \omega) x} d x =\frac{1}{\alpha-j \omega} \int_{-\infty}^{0} x d e^{(\alpha-j \omega) x} =\frac{1}{\alpha-j \omega} \left[xe^{(\alpha-j \omega) x}|_{-\infty} ^{0}-\frac{1}{\alpha-j \omega} \int_{-\infty}^{0} e^{(\alpha-j \omega) x} d ((\alpha -j \omega)x) \right]=\\ =\frac{1}{\alpha-j\omega}\left[0-\frac{1}{\alpha-j \omega}x e^{(\alpha-j \omega) x}|_{-\infty} ^{0}\right] =\frac{1}{\alpha-j \omega}\left[-\frac{1}{\alpha-j \omega}(1-0)\right]=\frac{-1}{(\alpha-j \omega)^{2}} $$

Note: $$\left.x e^{(\alpha-j \omega) x}\right|_{-\infty} ^{0} = 0 - \lim _{x \rightarrow-\infty} x e^{(\alpha-j \omega) x} = - \lim _{x \rightarrow-\infty} \frac {x}{e^{-(\alpha-j \omega)x}}= [\frac{\infty}{\infty}] = by~L'Hopital's~rule = - \lim _{x \rightarrow-\infty} \frac {1}{-(\alpha-j \omega)e^{-(\alpha-j \omega) x}} = 0$$

$$ I_{2}=\int_{0}^{+\infty} x e^{-(\alpha+j \omega) x} d x= -\frac{1}{\alpha+j \omega} \int_{0}^{+\infty} x d e^{-(\alpha+j \omega) x}= -\frac{1}{\alpha+j \omega}\left[\left.x e^{-(\alpha+j\omega)}|_{0} ^{+\infty}\right. +\frac{1}{\alpha+j \omega} \int_{0}^{+\infty} e^{-(\alpha+j \omega) x} d(-(\alpha+j \omega) x)\right]= \\ =-\frac{1}{(\alpha+j \omega)^{2}}(0-1)=\frac{1}{(\alpha+j \omega)^{2}} $$

Note: $$ \left.x e^{-(\alpha+j \omega) x}\right|_{0} ^{+\infty} = \lim _{x \rightarrow+\infty} x e^{-(\alpha+j \omega) x} - 0 = \lim _{x \rightarrow+\infty} \frac {x}{e^{(\alpha+j \omega)x}}= [\frac{\infty}{\infty}] = by~L'Hopital's~rule = \lim _{x \rightarrow+\infty} \frac {1}{(\alpha+j \omega)e^{(\alpha+j \omega) x}}= 0$$

Finally: $$I = I_{2} + I_{1} = \frac{1}{(\alpha+j \omega)^{2}} + \frac{-1}{(\alpha-j \omega)^{2}} = \frac{\alpha^{2}-2j\omega \alpha - \omega^{2}-\alpha^{2}-2j\omega +\omega^{2}}{(\alpha+j\omega) (\alpha + j\omega)(\alpha -j \omega)(\alpha - j \omega)} = \frac{-4j\omega\alpha}{\alpha^{2}+\omega^{2}}$$

Answer: $$F(\omega) = \frac{-4j\omega\alpha}{\alpha^{2}+\omega^{2}}$$

Task 1.b¶

Compute the Fourier transform for the function: $f(x) = e^{-a^{2}x^{2}}cos(bx)$

Here I will use the shifting property of FT. For this reason I will start my calculations from computing the FT of function: $f(x) = e^{-a^{2}x^{2}}$ and then will apply shift to the result.

$$F_{1}(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-a^{2} x^{2}} e^{-j \omega x}dx= \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-(a^{2} x^{2} + j \omega x -\frac{\omega^{2}}{4a^{2}}+\frac{\omega^{2}}{4a^{2}})} d x =e^{-\frac{\omega^{2}}{4a^{2}}} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-\left(|a| x+\frac{j \omega}{2|a|}\right)^{2}} d x=\\ =e^{-\frac{\omega^{2}}{4a^{2}}} \frac{1}{|a|} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-\left(|a| x+\frac{j\omega}{2|a|}\right)^{2}} d\left(|a| x+\frac{j \omega}{2|a|}\right) =\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{|a|} e^{-\frac{\omega^{2}}{4a^{2}}}$$

Applying shifting property:

$$F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-\alpha^{2} x^{2}} \cos (b x) e^{-j \omega x} d x=\frac{1}{2} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-a^{2} x^{2}}\left(e^{j b x}+e^{-j b x}\right) e^{-j \omega x} d x =\frac{1}{2}\left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-a^{2} x^{2}} e^{-j(\omega-b) x} d x+\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-a^{2} x^{2}} e^{-j(\omega+b) x} d x\right)= \\ =\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{|a|} e^{-\frac{(\omega-b)^{2}}{4 a^{2}}}+\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{|a|} e^{-\frac{(\omega+b)^{2}}{4 a^{2}}}\right) =\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2|a|}\left(e^{-\frac{(\omega-b)^{2}}{4 a^{2}}}+e^{-\frac{(\omega+ b)^{2}}{4 a^{2}}}\right)$$

Answer: $$F(\omega) = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2|a|}\left(e^{-\frac{(\omega-b)^{2}}{4 a^{2}}}+e^{-\frac{(\omega+ b)^{2}}{4 a^{2}}}\right)$$

First of all, I defined the cosine-filled sinc function: $f(x)=\frac{\sin (\pi x)}{\pi x} \cos \left(\omega_{0} x+\varphi_{0}\right)$. I write $\omega_{0}, \varphi_{0}$ because I want to derive the representation of cosine-filled sinc function in the frequency domain in general case.
By the way, it is obvious that for our case $\varphi_{0} = -\frac{\pi}{2}$ (because cosine is delayed ($f(0) = 0$)) and $\omega_{0} = 3\pi$
Proof: $f(x)=\frac{\sin (\pi x)}{\pi x} \cos (\omega_{0} x-\frac{\pi}{2}) = 0$,
then
$$\left[ \begin{gathered} sinc(x) = 0 \\ cos(\omega_{0}x - \frac{\pi}{2}) = 0 \\ \end{gathered} \right.$$ from the second equation we get
$$\omega_{0}x-\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{2} + \pi k, k \in N \\ \omega_{0}x = \pi + \pi k$$ Then according to the graph above I can say that when $x = \frac{1}{3}$ cosine-filled function equals to zero, so the first root (when $k = 0$) of the equation above can be find in this way: $$\frac{\omega_{0}}{3} = \pi \\ \omega_{0} = 3\pi$$

$$ F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} sinc(x) \cos \left(\omega_{0} x+\varphi_{0}\right) e^{-j \omega x} d x=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}sinc(x)\left(\frac{e^{j\left(\omega_{0} x+\varphi_{0}\right)}+e^{-j\left(\omega_{0} x+\varphi_{0}\right)}}{2}\right) \cdot e^{-j \omega x} d x= \\ =\frac{1}{2} \left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{j \varphi_{0}}sinc(x) e^{-j\left(\omega-\omega_{0}\right) x} d x+\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-j \varphi_{0}} sinc(x) e^{-j\left(\omega+\omega_{0}\right) x} d x\right)=I_{1}+I_{2} $$
$$ I_{1}=\frac{1}{2} e^{j \varphi_{0}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}sinc(x) e^{-j\left(\omega-\omega_{0}\right) x} d x=\left\{\begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{2} e^{j \varphi_{0}},\left|\omega-\omega_{0}\right| \leqslant \pi \\ 0, \text {otherwise} \hfill \end{array}\right. \\ I_{2}=\frac{1}{2} e^{-j \varphi_{0}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}sinc(x) e^{-j\left(\omega+\omega_{0}\right) x} d x=\left\{\begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{2} e^{-j \varphi_{0}},\left|\omega+\omega_{0}\right| \leqslant \pi \\ 0, \text {otherwise} \hfill \end{array}\right. $$

For our case $\omega_{0} = 3\pi, \varphi_{0} = -\frac{\pi}{2}$ we get: $$ I_{1}=\left\{\begin{array}{c} \frac{j}{2},2\pi \leqslant \omega \leqslant 4\pi \\ 0, \text {otherwise} \hfill \end{array}\right. \\ $$ $$ I_{2}=\left\{\begin{array}{c} -\frac{j}{2},-4\pi \leqslant \omega \leqslant -2\pi \\ 0, \text {otherwise} \hfill \end{array}\right. \\ $$

Finally: $$ I=\left\{\begin{array}{c} -\frac{j}{2},-4\pi \leqslant \omega \leqslant -2\pi \\ \frac{j}{2},2\pi \leqslant \omega \leqslant 4\pi \\ 0, \text {otherwise} \hfill \end{array}\right. \\ $$

Now let's plot the graph. Firstly, we need to find the magnitude of the signal in frequency domain. In our case it is obvious that $A = \frac{1}{2}$ (by definition $A(\omega) = \sqrt{F(\omega)F^{*}(\omega)}$ )

Compute (by hands) the convolution of the following signals:
h = [2, 3, 6, 8], x =[1, 2, 10, 1]

By definition of the discrete convolution: $$ (f * g)[n]=\sum_{m=-M}^{M} f[n-m] g[m] $$
Firstly I flip h signal: $$ h_{flipped} = [8, 6, 3, 2] $$
Then calculate the convolution:
1) Step 1: $$8~6~3~2 \\ ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~1~2~10~1 \\ 2 \cdot 1 = 2 $$ 2) Step 2: $$8~6~3~2 \\ ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~1~2~10~1 \\ 3 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 2= 3+4 = 7 $$ 3) Step 3: $$8~6~3~~2 \\ ~~~~~~~~1~2~10~1 \\ 6 \cdot 1 + 3 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 10= 6+6+20 = 32 $$ 4) Step 4: $$8~6~~3~~2 \\ 1~2~10~1 \\ 8 \cdot 1 + 6 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 10 + 2 \cdot 1= 8+12+30+2 = 52 $$ 5) Step 5: $$~~~~~~~~~8~~6~~3~~2 \\ ~~1~2~10~1 \\ 8 \cdot 2 + 6 \cdot 10 + 3 \cdot 1 = 16+60+3 = 79 $$ 6) Step 6: $$~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~8~~6~~3~~2 \\ ~~1~2~10~1 \\ 8 \cdot 10 + 6 \cdot 11 + 3 \cdot 1 = 80 + 6 = 86 $$ 7) Step 7: $$~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~8~~6~~3~~2 \\ ~~1~2~10~1 \\ 8 \cdot 1 = 8 $$ Answer: $conv = [2, 7, 32, 52, 79, 86, 8]$

The sinusoidal signal with the frequency $f = 6 kHz$ is represented by 4 signals periods sampled with the sample clock frequency $f_{s} = 15 kHz$. Are these samples enough to reconstruct the initial signal correctly.

I think that we can not reconstruct signal correctly based on these samples. Because when we sample function at frequence $f_{s}$ the sinusoidal functions yelds the same set of samples $\{sin(2\pi(f+Nf_{s})x + \varphi, N =0, \pm1, \pm2, \dots\}$ So without additional information we can't distinguish the frequency of the original function.
